Vermicomposting

Vermicomposting is the process of using earthworms (usually Eisenia fetida or red wigglers) to decompose organic waste like crop residues, cow dung, and kitchen waste into nutrient-rich compost. The final product, called vermicompost, is a dark, fine, soil-like material full of plant nutrients and beneficial microbes.

In India, many state agriculture/horticulture departments promote vermicomposting under organic farming schemes. Subsidies or free earthworms/tanks are sometimes given to farmers.

  • Using earthworms to turn farm waste into high-quality compost.
  • Very cheap, improves soil structure & fertility.
  • Farmers can sell compost as an extra source of income.

What is Vermicomposting?

A method of making organic manure using earthworms.

Earthworms eat crop residues, cow dung, and organic waste → they excrete it as vermicast, a nutrient-rich fertilizer.

Vermicompost improves soil fertility, water-holding capacity, and crop yield.

Materials Needed

  • Earthworms: Species like Eisenia fetida or local red worms.
  • Organic waste: Cow dung, crop residues, vegetable waste, leaf litter.
  • Moisture source: Water (preferably non-chlorinated).
  • Shady place: Avoid direct sun and heavy rain.
  • Tank/Pit/Bed: Brick tank, cement ring, or simple raised bed with shade.

Steps in Vermicomposting

1. Prepare the Bed/Pit

  • Size: Common is 3 m × 1 m × 1 m (can vary).
  • Shade: Provide with coconut leaves, thatch, or net.
  • Drainage: Ensure proper drainage to avoid waterlogging.

2. Pre-treatment of Waste

  • Mix cow dung with crop residues or organic waste.
  • Keep the mixture in a heap for 15–20 days to partially decompose (reduces heat).

3. Fill the Bed

  • Spread a layer of dried leaves or straw at the bottom.
  • Add 10–15 cm layer of pre-decomposed waste.
  • Release earthworms (about 1 kg worms per 100 kg waste).
  • Cover with moist gunny bags, straw, or banana leaves.

4. Maintain the Bed

  • Keep moisture at 60–70% (like a squeezed sponge).
  • Sprinkle water regularly, but avoid flooding.
  • Turn the material once every 15–20 days for aeration.

5. Harvesting

  • Compost is ready in 45–60 days.
  • It looks dark brown/black, granular, and odorless.
  • Separate worms by keeping compost in heaps under sunlight — worms move down, compost can be collected from top.

Nutrient Value of Vermicompost

  • Contains NPK: Nitrogen, Phosphorus, and Potassium in available form.
  • Micronutrients: Also rich in Calcium (Ca), Magnesium (Mg), Iron (Fe), and Zinc (Zn).
  • Soil Benefits: Improves soil microbial activity and structure.

Vermicompost vs Chemical Fertilizer

Aspect Vermicompost Chemical Fertilizer
Cost Low cost, can be produced on-farm High recurring expense
Nutrients Contains NPK + micronutrients (Ca, Mg, Fe, Zn) Mostly NPK only, limited micronutrients
Soil Health Improves structure, microbial activity, water retention May cause soil degradation with long-term use
Environmental Impact Eco-friendly, no pollution Leaching & pollution of soil/water
Crop Quality Better taste, nutrition, and shelf life Higher yield but possible decline in quality
Availability Locally available, but limited quantity Readily available in market

Applications of Vermicompost

  • Field Crops: Apply 2–3 tons per hectare along with reduced chemical fertilizer for cereals, pulses, and oilseeds.
  • Vegetables: Use 1–2 kg per plant at sowing or transplanting; improves yield and taste.
  • Fruit Trees: Apply 5–10 kg per tree, twice a year (before flowering and after fruiting).
  • Pot & Garden Plants: Mix 200–500 g with potting soil or apply as top dressing.
  • Nurseries: Excellent medium for raising seedlings; enhances root development.

Advantages

  • Low cost & eco-friendly: Acts as a natural manure without harming the environment.
  • Soil health: Improves fertility, structure, and overall crop quality.
  • Reduced chemical use: Cuts down fertilizer requirement by 25–50%.
  • Additional income: Can be sold in the market at ₹5–10 per kg.

Precautions

  • No harmful waste: Do not use plastics, metals, or diseased plant material.
  • Avoid fresh dung: It generates heat and can kill worms — only use pre-decomposed material.
  • Protection: Safeguard the bed from ants, termites, rats, and birds.
  • Moisture control: Keep at 60–70% — not too dry, not waterlogged.
