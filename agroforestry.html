Agroforestry

Agroforestry is a farming system where trees or shrubs are grown alongside crops and/or livestock on the same piece of land. Instead of keeping trees and crops separate, farmers integrate them to get multiple benefits.

Common Agroforestry Practices in India

  • Alley cropping: Crops grown between rows of trees.
  • Boundary planting: Trees planted on farm boundaries with crops inside.
  • Silvipasture: Trees + grasses for fodder and livestock.
  • Agri-horticulture: Fruit trees + seasonal crops.

Why Use It?

  • Enhanced Productivity: Combines trees, crops, and/or livestock for diversified output.
  • Soil Health Improvement: Trees improve nutrient cycling, reduce erosion, and increase organic matter.
  • Microclimate Regulation: Trees provide shade, windbreaks, and reduce temperature extremes.
  • Income Diversification: Farmers can earn from timber, fruits, fodder, or other tree products.
  • Environmental Benefits: Carbon sequestration, biodiversity conservation, and improved water management.

Types of Agroforestry Systems

A. Agrisilviculture (Crops + Trees)

  • Example: Wheat, maize, or pulses grown between rows of poplar, eucalyptus, or neem.
  • Benefit: Annual crop income + timber income.

B. Silvipasture (Trees + Pasture/Grass)

  • Example: Leucaena or Subabul trees with Napier grass for fodder.
  • Benefit: Livestock feed + fuelwood.

C. Agri-Horticulture (Crops + Fruit Trees)

  • Example: Vegetables or pulses with mango, guava, or citrus orchards.
  • Benefit: Fruit income + crop returns.

D. Boundary/Alley Planting

  • Example: Trees planted along field bunds (eucalyptus, neem, moringa).
  • Benefit: Shelterbelt, fodder, fencing material.

E. Agro-Silvi-Pastoral

  • Example: Combination of crops + trees + animals (integrated system).
  • Benefit: Multiple outputs: crops, timber, fodder, and livestock products.

Benefits of AgroForestry

  • Income diversification: Regular returns from crops, long-term timber/fruit profits.
  • Soil fertility improvement: Nitrogen-fixing trees enrich soil (e.g., Gliricidia, Subabul).
  • Water conservation: Tree roots improve infiltration and reduce runoff.
  • Microclimate regulation: Shade reduces soil temperature; windbreaks protect crops.
  • Biodiversity & sustainability: Supports birds, bees, and beneficial organisms.
  • Resilience to climate change: Reduces risk by spreading income sources.

Tree Species Suitable for Farmers

  • Timber/Fuelwood: Eucalyptus, Poplar, Teak, Neem, Subabul (Leucaena).
  • Fruit Trees: Mango, Guava, Citrus, Amla, Custard apple.
  • Nitrogen-Fixing Trees: Sesbania, Gliricidia, Subabul.
  • Fodder Trees: Moringa, Leucaena, Albizia.

How to Start Agroforestry

  • Select the right system: Based on land size, water availability, and market demand.
  • Choose compatible tree species: Avoid species that shade crops excessively in early years.
  • Spacing: Wide spacing for timber (5–8 m), closer for fruit trees (6–10 m depending on species).
  • Crop choice: Short-duration, shade-tolerant crops are best (pulses, vegetables, fodder).
  • Management:
    • Prune trees to avoid excessive shading.
    • Use tree leaves for compost/mulch.
    • Rotate crops to maintain soil fertility.

Government & Institutional Support

In India, agroforestry is promoted under the National Agroforestry Policy (2014).

  • Seedlings: Free or subsidized tree seedlings for farmers.
  • Technical training: Guidance on agroforestry practices and management.
  • Market linkages: Support for selling timber and fruit products.
  • Carbon credits: Agroforestry is linked with carbon credit opportunities in some regions.

Farmer’s Quick Checklist

  • Start with a small portion of land.
  • Use fast-growing trees (eucalyptus, subabul) along with fruit trees (mango, guava).
  • Combine with short-duration crops (vegetables, pulses).
  • Use tree leaves as mulch or compost.
  • Connect with local agriculture/horticulture department for saplings and support.
