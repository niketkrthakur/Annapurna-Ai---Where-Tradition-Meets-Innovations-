Micro Irrigation Techniques

Micro irrigation is a modern method of watering crops that delivers water directly to the root zone of plants in small, controlled amounts. It includes techniques like drip irrigation (water drips slowly through pipes and emitters) and sprinkler/micro-sprinkler systems (water sprayed in fine droplets).

Micro irrigation is especially useful for vegetables, orchards, cash crops, and water-scarce regions. There are multiple subsidies by the Government to help farmers adopt it.

  • Delivers water directly to plant roots (drip).
  • Saves up to 60–70% of water compared to traditional methods.
  • Efficient in drought-prone areas.

Why Use It?

Saves water, increases yield, reduces weed growth, supports fertigation, and works well in water-scarce areas.

Site & Crop Information

Crop type & spacing:

  • Row crops: Cotton, maize, sugarcane → drip lines along each row.
  • Orchards: Mango, banana, citrus → drippers around the tree basin.
  • Vegetables: Tomato, chili, cucumber → 1 or 2 lateral lines per bed.
  • Flowers: Rose, marigold → closely spaced laterals.

Plant water requirement:

  • Changes with growth stage. For example, tomato needs less water at seedling stage and much more at fruiting stage.
  • Farmers can follow evapotranspiration (ET) charts from local agriculture departments to plan daily irrigation.

Soil type:

  • Sandy soil: Frequent irrigation in small doses (water drains quickly).
  • Clay soil: Apply slowly to avoid waterlogging.
  • Loam soil: Best for drip, moderate scheduling.

Field topography:

  • Flat fields are easiest.
  • Slopes require pressure regulators or terrace layout.

Water source & availability:

  • Borewell, canal, reservoir, tank, pond.
  • Continuous and reliable supply is important; backup source is useful in dry months.

Water Quality

Chemical quality:

  • Salinity (EC): High salts affect crop growth.
  • Hardness (Ca, Mg): Causes scaling in pipes.
  • pH: Keep between 6.5–8.0.

Physical impurities:

  • Sand, silt, and algae clog filters and drippers.
  • Filtration is compulsory if water is not clear.

Biological issues:

  • Surface water may contain algae, bacteria, or organic matter.
  • Chlorination or periodic flushing is needed.

Remember: Poor water quality = clogged emitters → uneven irrigation → reduced yield.

System Components

  • Head unit:
    • Pump to lift water.
    • Filters (sand, screen, or disc) to remove impurities.
    • Fertilizer tank/venturi for applying fertilizer with irrigation (fertigation).
  • Mainline & sub-main pipes: Carry water from head unit to laterals.
  • Laterals: Small pipes (12–20 mm) laid near crops.
  • Emitters/Drippers: Control the water flow (e.g., 2 L/hr).
  • Valves & regulators: For controlling flow, pressure, and section-wise irrigation.

Design Principles

  • Operating pressure: Normally 0.7–2.5 bar (10–30 psi) for drip.
  • Emitter spacing & discharge rate:
    • Vegetables: 30–40 cm spacing, 2 L/hr discharge.
    • Orchards: 4–8 L/hr emitters placed near tree basin.
  • Hydraulic design: Proper pipe sizing to ensure uniform flow across the field.
  • Automation options: Timers, soil moisture sensors, and mobile-based control systems can save time and labor.

Installation & Operation

  • Lay out pipes: As per crop geometry.
  • Laterals: Fix on soil surface or slightly bury for protection.
  • Flushing: Flush lines regularly to remove dirt.
  • Irrigation scheduling:
    • Seedling: Short time, low water.
    • Flowering/Fruiting: Longer time, higher water.

Maintenance

  • Filter & pipeline flushing: Flush weekly (or as per water quality).
  • Treatments: Apply acid/chlorine if scaling or algae growth is seen.
  • Emitters: Check for uniform discharge; replace clogged ones.
  • Repairs & protection: Repair leaks quickly and protect laterals from rats or farm equipment damage.

Economics & Support

  • Cost–Benefit:
    • Initial installation cost per hectare: approx. ₹40,000–₹70,000 (varies by crop and layout).
    • Savings: 30–60% water, 20–40% fertilizer, 30–40% labor.
    • Yield increase: 20–50% depending on crop.
  • Government Subsidies:
    • In India, 45–70% subsidy is available under PMKSY and state schemes.
    • Small/marginal farmers often get higher subsidy.
  • Long-term benefits:
    • Better water-use efficiency.
    • Higher yields and improved crop quality.
    • Payback period usually 1.5–3 years.
